\begin{abstract}

This paper investigates whether tonotactic learnability differs across 
representational choices. We conduct an experiment by using the same 
dataset but over two different representations: string and autosegmental 
representations (ARs). Two learning models are used: Maximum Entropy 
(MaxEnt) model and the Bottom-Up Factor Inference Algorithm (BUFIA), 
allowing us to examine whether differences in learning outcomes arise from 
the learning model as well as from the representation. Using identical 
training and testing datasets across conditions, we evaluate five 
implemented combinations: Baseline-Str, Baseline-AR, MaxEnt-Str, BUFIA-Str, 
and BUFIA-AR. The results show that (1) for the baseline grammar, which is 
static and surface-based, representational choice does not affect learning 
outcomes; and (2) for computationally learned grammars, performance is 
strongest when grammars are induced over ARs under a graph-based 
categorical learner. These findings suggest that representation plays an 
important role in tonotactic learning.
mechanisms.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

This paper focuses on the learning of tonal phonotactics, or tonotactics, which addresses the question of the well-formedness of how tones and tone-bearing units (TBUs, such as syllable or mora) are organized in tonal languages, and explores how tonal representations can influence learning outcomes.
For a long time, tonotactics has received less attention in computational learning than segmental phonotactics, despite the widespread distribution of tonal languages across the world \citep{yip2002tone}.
One possible reason for this gap lies in representational differences.
Segmental phonotactics is typically modeled using linear representations such as strings, which makes it readily compatible with a wide range of existing learning models.
Tonotactics, by contrast, has long been analyzed over non-linear structures, most widely used autosegmental representations \citep[ARs,][]{goldsmith1976autosegmental}.
This representational challenge has led to a dearth of computational models that operate directly over non-linear structures, with many approaches instead flattening tonal patterns into strings to fit linear learning frameworks.
Although a substantial body of work has argued that linear representations are inadequate for capturing tonal generalizations \citep{clements2010autosegmental, oddenintroducing2013}, and other studies have examined the computational properties of ARs \citep{bird1990phonological,coleman1991no, kornai2007mathematical,jardine2015concatenation,jardine2016locality, jardine2017LocalNature,jardine2019expressivity}, we are unaware of any empirical demonstrations of clear advantages for non-linear representations over strings in tonotactic learning.

In this paper, we address two questions: (1) how effectively tonotactics can be learned over ARs, and (2) whether ARs improve learning outcomes compared with alternative string-based representations.
To address these questions, we encoded the same dataset in three ways (strings, sequences of features, ARs) and applied versions of the Bottom-Up Factor Inference Algorithm (BUFIA) \citep{chandleeBufia2019} and the Maximum Entropy phonotactic learning model \citep{hayes2008maximum} to these datasets.
A set of linguistically motivated grammars, also encoded accroding to these three representations, helps serve as a baseline. 

One limitation of this study is that the current implementation of MaxEnt does not support ARs.
A more comprehensive study would also include a version of MaxEnt which can operate over ARs.
Despite this limitation, the results presented here help assess whether ARs contribute to learning.

\citet{li2025learning}. The corpus contains 621 monomorphemic,

transcribed in orthography.

This corpus was divided into four partitions (folds).
Each fold was converted into the three representational schemes.
The tonal representations studied here abstract away from segmental content and retain only tonal and TBU information.
For example, the Hausa word \textit{mù.tûm} (``person'') is represented in one of the three ways as shown in Table~\ref{fig:reps}. Representations are discussed in detail in \S\ref{sec:background}.

For each representation and model, cross-fold validation was conducted and their model performance examined.
In every case, the evaluation set was augmented to include word-forms of matched sizes that violated the constraints in the baseline grammar in order to  meaningfully report precision, recall, and F1 scores, which helps identify whether that the models generalize and not overgeneralize.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

This section provides the theoretical and computational background and
methods for the different representations investigated here.
We adopt a model-theoretic perspective that treats both representations as
different types of mathematical objects \citep{enderton2001mathematical}, or more specifically, relational structures
\citep{rogers2013,chandleeBufia2019,rogers.lambert2019mol}.

\subsection{Representations}

We define a phonological representation as relational structures of the 
form $\langle D, \mathcal{R} \rangle$, where $D$ is a finite domain of 
positions, and $\mathcal{R}$ is a set of relations (also known as the
model \textit{signature}) that connects the composing units of the 
phonological representation \citep{strother2017using, jardine2016locality}.
Relations may be unary, 
indicating the identity of an element, or binary, specifying the 
relationship between pairs of elements. 

A string representation arranges symbols along a single axis.
The signature of a string model includes unary  labeling relations indicating the symbols, and a single binary successor relation $\lhd$ defining linear order.
For example, \textit{L.HL} can be  written and visualized as in Figure~\ref{sec2.fig.string}. The domain 
$D=\{1,2,3,4\}$ specifies four positions, labeled by tone and syllable 
boundary marker (.), and the successor relation encodes temporal order. The 
structural information (e.g., each tone occupying a mora) is encoded 
implicitly in the unary relations, for example by adding a boundary marker, 
or by allowing each symbol to implicitly represent one mora connected with 
one tone. For more complex tonal forms where a single TBU is associated with 
multiple tones, special symbols must be introduced into the alphabet, such 
as R (representing LH on one mora) or F (representing HL on one mora).

\input{string.model.tex}

ARs, in contrast, correspond to multi-tiered relational structures. The 
signature includes a successor relation but over multiple tiers 
(e.g., tone, mora, syllable). Associations across tiers are captured by an 
association relation $\alpha$. In this representation, structural 
dependencies such as tone spreading or contour formation are encoded 
directly as relations rather than inferred from symbol sequences. 

For example, in Figure~\ref{armodel}, the AR consists of eight units, 
defined by $D=\{1,\ldots,8\}$, arranged across three tiers, each following 
a successor relation $\lhd=\{(1,2),(2,3),\ldots\}$. The association 
relations, on the other hand, are expressed as a set of unordered pairs 
$\alpha=\{(1,4),(2,5),\ldots,(6,8)\}$, indicating which two elements are 
connected by an association line.

\input{AR.model.tex}

This formalization explicitly captures how within-tier and across-tier 
relations are represented in autosegmental phonology 
\citep{goldsmith1976autosegmental}. In particular, tonal generalizations 
have been argued to be computationally simpler over ARs than over strings, 
because cross-tier dependencies are represented directly as relations. This 
allows processes to be modeled by modifying associations rather than 
altering feature values or the alphabet, and can describe long-distance 
processes locally \citep{goldsmith1976autosegmental,jardine2017LocalNature}.

\subsection{Phonotactic Learning Models}

The main model used in this paper is based on the Bottom-Up 
Factor Inference Algorithm (BUFIA; \citet{chandleeBufia2019}). BUFIA takes 
on model-theoretic representations, thereby gives a flexibility of specific 
linguistic representations. In this work, we adopted two versions of BUFIA,
BUFIA-Str and BUFIA-AR.

In a nutshell, BUFIA is a categorical phonotactic learner that traverses a 
hypothesis space of all logically possible structures. These structures, 
which serve as candidate constraints, are partially ordered by a containment 
relation: some pairs of structures stand in a containment relation (one 
containing the other), while others are not comparable. As illustrated in 
Figure [maybe??] for strings over the alphabet ${a,b}$, more general 
structures (e.g., the empty string or singletons) occupy lower positions in 
the space, whereas more specific structures appear higher. For any given 
structure, those that properly contain it are its \textit{superfactors} 
(positioned above), and those that it properly contains are its 
\textit{subfactors} (positioned below). Among the superfactors, only those 
that are one level above the current candidate are referred to as its 
\textit{next superfactors}.

Two core operations of BUFIA determine its upward search or pruning: 
containment and the sub/superfactor relation. The learner is provided with a 
set of positive data and a complexity bound $k$. The search begins 
from an initial structure (typically the empty structure). For each 
candidate, BUFIA checks whether it is contained in the positive dataset. 
If it is attested, the learner generates its next superfactors and continues 
searching upward. Otherwise, the structure is identified as a forbidden 
factor (i.e., a constraint), and prune off its superfactors from space for 
not checking. In this way, BUFIA identifies the most general unattested 
structures and prunes all more specific structures that would necessarily 
also be forbidden. The resulting grammar guarantees full coverage of the 
observed data while remaining maximally general.

As said before, a key merit of BUFIA is its representational flexibility. 
The algorithm can operate over any representational choices, provided they 
are defined model-theoretically. Recent work has demonstrated the success of 
BUFIA in phonotactic learning. \citet{Swanson+2025} implemented a 
string-based version of BUFIA to Quechua phonotactics and showed that it 
captures the previous empirical generalizations. \citet{li2025learning} 
introduced a version of BUFIA that operates directly over ARs for tonotactic 
learning. This representational flexibility allows controlled comparison of 
different representations of the same data under a shared learning framework.

As noted above, BUFIA is a categorical learner: a candidate structure is 
either well-formed or ill-formed, depending on whether it contains any 
forbidden subfactor. In contrast, probabilistic models assign gradient 
well-formedness values. A representative of such models is the Maximum 
Entropy phonotactic learner \citep[MaxEnt;][]{hayes2008maximum}. MaxEnt 
interprets well-formedness as probability rather than a binary value and 
treats constraints as violable, each associated with a penalty weight. The 
well-formedness of a candidate is computed from the sum of its  
constraint violations. This evaluation is cumulative: the more constraints a 
candidate violates, and the higher the weights of those constraints, the 
lower its predicted probability of well-formedness.

MaxEnt has been widely used in string-based phonotactic learning, and more 
recent implementations can induce phonological tiers to capture non-local 
patterns \citep{wilson2018accidental,GouskovaGallagher2020}. 
\citet{lee2025representing} propose a MaxEnt-based learner for stress 
patterns that reflects metrical structure. These studies suggest that 
probabilistic string-based approaches can learn structural regularities that 
are not directly visible in surface sequences. However, these tier-based 
representations differ from ARs in that they typically do not encode 
association relations between individual elements on separate tiers.

\section{Experiment: Hausa Tonotactics}

To test tonotactic learnability across representations and models, 
we conducted a controlled computational experiment on the same dataset. 
In this section, we detail the experimental setup, including the 
dataset over two formats, the construction of test data, and 
the evaluation metrics used to compare model performance.

\subsection{Hausa Data}

The language data used in this study come from Hausa, a major tonal 
language spoken in West Africa. We use a curated corpus from 
\citet{li2025learning}, constructed from the Hausa database 
\citep{wold-4} in the \textit{World Loanword Database}. The corpus 
contains 621 monomorphemic, non-borrowed words (64 types) from the 
core vocabulary, transcribed in orthography.

As background, Hausa has three surface tones in orthography: H, L,
and HL (a falling tone, which occurs only in heavy syllables). 
Previously proposed generalizations about Hausa tonotactics are listed 
in Table~\ref{rules} \citep{Newmanbook,leben1996tonal,zoll2003optimal}.


\input{constraints.table.tex}

Among the constraints in Table~\ref{rules}, the first three constraints,
\textsc{*Rise$_\sigma$},\textsc{*Contour$_{\mu}$},\textsc{*3T}, are hard
constraints that define the basic phonology of tones and syllable structures 
of Hausa. Constraint \textsc{*NonFinHL} applies to the non-derived Hausa words 
where the falling contour (HL) can only occur word finally. The constraint 
\textsc{*Lapse} is proposed by \citet{zoll2003optimal} that explain the rarity 
of L tone spreading. \citet{Newmanbook} points out that there are exceptional 
words that violate the last two constraints. 

We classify the data into two groups. Positive items are drawn directly 
from the corpus and are therefore attested. Negative items consist of 
self-constructed forms that are unattested and each violates at least 
one hard constraint listed in Table \ref{rules}. All data converted into three 
systems of representations: string, feature, and ARs. One positive and one
nonce form are given in the Table \ref{tab.threereps}.
\input{reps.examples.tex}

The segment and feature representations are based on the orthographic form, 
where each TBU is represented by a single symbol or a feature bundle. When 
multiple tones map onto a single TBU, such as monomoraic LH or monomoraic 
HL, these are represented as distinct symbols in the string alphabet (R and F, 
respectively). For the feature system, we adopt a two-feature specification: \{L, 
Contour\}. Level tones are specified as either [+L] or [−L], while contour tones 
are specified as [+Contour] in addition to their initial tone value. For example, 
in Table \ref{tab.threereps}, the Hausa word \textit{mù.tûm} ‘person’ is 
represented as \textit{L.HL} in the string format, and its feature 
representation is [+L].[−L][+Contour]. A nonce word such as \textit{R.H} 
would be represented in the feature system as [+L, +Contour].[−L, −Contour].

Unlike the string representation, AR does not require expanding the tonal
inventory to include symbols such as R and F. Instead, mora and syllables ($
\mu,\sigma$) are included in the alphabet. Consequently, syllable boundaries 
are naturally encoded. In the implementation, each AR is 
encoded as a pair of adjacency matrices, which we do not discuss further here.

\subsection{Procedure}

The experiment compares grammars learned under multiple conditions, varying both 
learning model (BUFIA vs.\ MaxEnt) and representation (Segment, Feature, AR). 
These learned grammars are evaluated against baseline grammars defined over the 
corresponding representations (Baseline-Seg/Ftr/AR). 
Table~\ref{tab.all.models} summarizes all model conditions.

We set a stopping threshold that limits the maximum length or structural complexity 
of learned constraints for two reasons. First, all computational models require such 
a bound as a stopping condition; for MaxEnt, this limit cannot exceed 4. Second, 
to ensure comparability with the baseline grammar, we aim to match the size of 
learned constraints to the most complex constraint in the baseline. For string 
representations, the most complex baseline constraint has length 3 (e.g., *HL.).

For AR representations, matching complexity is less straightforward, since the 
threshold consists of three parameters: $t$ (maximum number of tonal units), 
$s$ (maximum number of syllables), and $m$ (maximum number of moras). To facilitate
cross-representation and cross-model comparison, we align the AR thresholds with 
the size of the maximal baseline AR constraints, setting $t=2$, $s=2$, and $m=3$.

\subsection{Evaluation}
We conducted a 4-fold cross-validation procedure, partitioning the dataset into 
four equally sized folds (16 per fold, 64 in total). In each iteration, 
three folds were used for training and the remaining fold served as the 
test set. This process was repeated four times, with each fold acting 
as the test set once. Each test set also include self-constructed negative 
forms whose size matched the size of the held-out positive set
(16 types), yielding a total of 32 test types per test fold. 

%?For the frequency-sensitive models, evaluation was 
%conducted over token data. Each fold contained 159 tokens, and the corresponding 
%test fold was augmented with an equal number of negative tokens, resulting in 318 
%tokens per test set.

To evaluate model performance, we compared learning outcomes to the gold 
standard (the prediction by the baseline grammar). A prediction was counted 
as correct if the model/baseline accepted attested forms (true positives) 
and rejected constructed negative forms (true negatives). Performance was
measured using averaged recall, precision, and F1 score across folds.

It should be noted that the MaxEnt model produces a gradient grammar in which 
constraints are violable and associated with weights. To evaluate the accuracy 
and error patterns of MaxEnt, we use two well-formedness criteria against 
the gold standard: (1) Above-zero: a form is classified as ill-formed if 
its total weighted violation score is greater than zero. This practice follows 
\citet{wilson2018accidental}; (2) Above-average: a form is treated as ill-formed 
if its total weighted violation score exceeds the average violation score 
across all candidate forms. These two criteria affect the categorical 
interpretation of a probabilistic grammar. We report the metrics under 
both evaluation criteria to choose the better assessment.

\section{Results}

\input{results1.tex}
First, a comparison among Baseline-Seg, Baseline-Ftr, and Baseline-AR
shows that their results are identical. When the same grammar is 
applied across the three representations, it produces the same result 
on the dataset. A closer inspection of the errors confirms that they 
coincide. This indicates that, given a one-to-one correspondence 
between representations, representational differences alone do not 
affect the behavior of a fixed grammar over surface forms. However, 
this equivalence depends on constructing the alphabets of the three 
systems to ensure a bijective mapping.

For the learned grammars, performance varies depending on both the 
representation and the learning model. Under the over-zero evaluation 
criterion, the MaxEnt models show better performance with the feature 
representation: MaxEnt-Ftr achieves an F1 score of 0.650, whereas 
MaxEnt-Seg reaches 0.466. This suggests that learning is more 
effective under the feature representation in this condition.
However, under the over-average evaluation criterion, performance is 
higher overall and shows an opposite representational choice: 
MaxEnt-Ftr achieves an F1 score of 0.832, while MaxEnt-Seg reaches 
0.871, with the segment representation performing slightly better 
in this case. 

Among the BUFIA grammars, no difference is observed between segment 
and feature representations; both achieve an F1 score of 0.992, 
outperforming both MaxEnt and the baseline. When BUFIA is applied 
to AR representations, performance reaches the ceiling (F1 = 1.000). 

In a summary, the current experiment shows the learning outcome 
ranking as BUFIA-AR > BUFIA-Str/Ftr > MaxEnt-Seg (over 
average) > MaxEnt-Ftr (over average) > Baseline-Seg/Ftr/Ar >
MaxEnt-Ftr (over zero) > MaxEnt-Seg (over zero). 

One finding is AR provide a learning advantage over segmental or 
feature-based representations. With respect to the difference 
between segments and features, the present results do not reveal 
a clear advantage for either representation: under BUFIA, two 
representations yield identical performance while in MaxEnt the
results are inconsistent across evaluation criteria.

This raises another question: how should the well-formedness 
threshold be determined for gradient learners? The evaluation 
criterion substantially affects MaxEnt’s performance. A 
stricter threshold (e.g., the “over-zero” criterion) yields 
poorer results than a more lenient threshold. A greater attention 
should be paid to how well-formedness is defined when evaluating 
probabilistic phonotactic learners.

\subsection{Beyond Baseline: varying complexity}
We next ask a further question: beyond representational format, 
what factors contribute to tonotactic learning performance to 
gradient or categorical learners?

One factor that may influence gradient learners is frequency 
information. The weights that MaxEnt assigns to candidate 
constraints depend heavily on the frequency of attested forms. 
In the previous experiments, the model was trained on types 
rather than tokens; therefore, frequency information was not 
available during learning. 

In a follow-up experiment, we reintroduced frequency information 
to MaxEnt, and used token data rather than type data. As in the 
previous setup, the evaluation includes both held-out positive forms 
and an equal number of constructed negative forms in the test set, 
allowing recall, precision, and F1 scores to be computed.

Another factor concerns the stopping threshold. An important issue in grammar induction 
is how to determine an appropriate complexity bound that accounts for the data without 
leading to overgeneralization. Different stopping conditions may substantially 
influence the constraints learned and the resulting generalization patterns. 
To address this, we set the maximum constraint length in MaxEnt to 4 (the highest 
value permitted by the current implementation) and compare results across different 
complexity bounds within this limit. For BUFIA, we conduct a comprehensive evaluation 
across multiple values of $k$. ALl results are listed in Table \ref{tab:all_models} 
except for BUFIA-AR, which is listed in Table \ref{tab:ar_grid} for better
presentation. 

\input{results2.tex}
From Table~\ref{tab:all_models}, we see that the inclusion of frequency information substantially improves MaxEnt performance, with F1 scores reaching 0.990 for both segment and feature representations. This suggests that frequency information can help capture phonological patterns in MaxEnt.

As for the stopping threshold, increasing the maximum constraint length from 3 to 4 does not significantly affect MaxEnt performance, indicating that the model is robust to this parameter within the tested range. 

For BUFIA, as the complexity bound increases, performance gradually decreases. This usually due to overfitting or overgeneralization but even at higher complexity bounds, BUFIA maintains high precision, suggesting that it effectively captures the core phonotactic constraints without overgeneralizing. A significant drop in recall occurs at $k=6$ and $k=7$, indicating that the model is starting to overfit the training data. 

When looking at the AR-based BUFIA, we see that across parameter settings,
the recall rate is consistently high, and precision increase when the mora 
count is over 3, or when the tone count is greater than 1.  Epscially,
when the t = {2,3}, s ={1,2} , m =3; the model achieves perfect precision and high recall, leading to an F1 score of 1.000. This suggests that the AR representation allows BUFIA to effectively capture the relevant phonotactic constraints without overfitting, even as the complexity threshold increases.

remain consistent across representations. Besides, as mentioned earlier, the
two 
systems rely on different alphabets, that is, different basic units of
phonological 
representation. One could ask how these units are determined and whether they
can be 
supported by computational learning or by psycholinguistic evidence.

Beyond the baseline grammar, comparisons among computationally learned, dynamic

grammars show that the two BUFIA learners differ in performance. Learning over
ARs 
achieves higher accuracy on attested forms without increased
overgeneralization. This 
pattern remains robust even when the dataset includes rare forms.

The relatively low performance of the MaxEnt model may raise questions. One
possible 
explanation is the limited dataset size. This
study used type-based rather than token-based data. 
Without large dataset or frequency information, the 
MaxEnt performance may be limited. In addition, we 
interpret all MaxEnt constraints categorically: any 
non-zero weighted violation sum, even if very 
small, is treated as a violation. Future work could 
explore how to determine well-formedness thresholds 
more systematically.

In conclusion, we show that tonotactics can be learned effectively and
efficiently over ARs using BUFIA-AR, even without frequency information. This
highlights a future direction for grammar inference in tonal languages. For
example, many tonal processes (input–output mappings) involve changes in
association relations rather than changes in symbols, which makes learning over
multi-tier structures more necessary. More generally, adopting linguistically
interpretable learning models  
or representations will also help distinguish among competing theories of
linguistic representation and generalization.
