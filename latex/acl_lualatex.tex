\begin{abstract}
This paper investigates whether phonological learning primarily arises from the data representation (string vs. autosegmental representations) or from the learning model (probabilistic vs. categorical). We compare two learning frameworks: a Maximum Entropy (MaxEnt) model and BUFIA, a categorical learner adapted to operate over ARs. Using the same training and testing datasets across conditions, we systematically evaluate four combinations: MaxEnt-Ftr, BUFIA-Ftr, and BUFIA-AR. The results show that learning performance is strongest when grammars are induced over ARs, particularly when paired with a categorical learner. These findings suggest that representational structure plays a role in learning success than probabilistic weighting, and that categorical structure-sensitive learners benefit most from autosegmental representations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Phonotactic learning has long been a central topic in computational phonology. Despite tone being a core component of many linguistic systems, the computational learning of tonal patterns has received comparatively less attention. One reason for this gap may lie in representational differences. Segmental phonology is typically modeled using linear representations such as strings or feature bundles, and a wide range of existing learning models are designed to operate over such formats. Tonal grammars, however, depend on non-linear structure. Tonal patterns are commonly analyzed using autosegmental representations (ARs), which encode tones on separate tiers and capture association relations between tones and prosodic units such as moras or syllables [cite].

Linear representational formats have proven effective for modeling segmental phonotactics, but they have been argued to be inadequate for tonal systems. Tonal phenomena frequently involve non-local dependencies, spreading processes, floating tones, and tier-based interactions. When such structure is collapsed into a linear string, critical generalizations may be obscured. As a result, learners operating over strings may face a substantially larger or less structured hypothesis space than learners operating over representations that explicitly encode tonal associations [cite; e.g., Odden].

This raises a broader question about the source of inductive bias in tonal learning. Is learning success primarily determined by the representational format, or by the learning model itself? To address this question, this paper introduces an adaptation of the Bottom-Up Factorial Inference Algorithm (BUFIA) [cite], a categorical grammar induction algorithm, to operate directly over autosegmental representations. We then compare learning outcomes across two representational formats (string vs. AR) and two learning paradigms (categorical vs. probabilistic).

Specifically, we evaluate three conditions: string-based learning and AR-based learning, each paired with either a categorical learner (BUFIA) or a probabilistic Maximum Entropy (MaxEnt) learner using strings. Admittedly, it

\subsection{Models}
As mentioned above, we use two learning models: BUFIA and MaxEnt. Both models take as input a set of positive data and return a grammar. The two models differ in their learning mechanisms. BUFIA is a categorical learning model and has been implemented to operate over both autosegmental representations (ARs) and strings. MaxEnt, by contrast, is a statistical model that extracts constraints and assigns weights to them, evaluating forms based on their harmonic scores under a maximum entropy framework.

We performed four-fold cross-validation over the set of attested types. In each fold, the training set consists of 48 positive forms. The evaluation involves two test sets.

The first test set includes the remaining held-out positive data. To evaluate overgeneration, we constructed a matching set of negative forms. The sixteen negative forms each violate one or two pre-established constraints listed in Tables 1 and 2. As with the training data, the constructed negative forms are represented both as strings and as ARs (see Table X). Test Set 1 therefore contains 32 types in total (16 positive and 16 negative). Performance is evaluated by measuring whether the learned grammar correctly recognizes the well-formed positive data and rejects the negative forms.

The second test set builds on the first by adding four exceptional positive types, along with four matched negative forms, resulting in a total of 40 types. The exceptional forms are drawn from Hausa data reported by Newman and violate both *Clash and *Nonfinal-Fall. Newman notes that there are eight such examples in the corpus and suggests that they may have historical explanations, though the exact source of these exceptions remains unclear.

The averaged results across folds are shown in Tables 4 and 5. For both test sets, all models under both representational formats achieved perfect precision (1.000).
