\begin{abstract}

This paper investigates whether tonotactic learnability differs across 
representational choices. We conduct an experiment by using the same 
dataset but over two different representations: string and autosegmental 
representations (ARs). Two learning models are used: Maximum Entropy 
(MaxEnt) model and the Bottom-Up Factor Inference Algorithm (BUFIA), 
allowing us to examine whether differences in learning outcomes arise from 
the learning model as well as from the representation. Using identical 
training and testing datasets across conditions, we evaluate five 
implemented combinations: Baseline-Str, Baseline-AR, MaxEnt-Str, BUFIA-Str, 
and BUFIA-AR. The results show that (1) for the baseline grammar, which is 
static and surface-based, representational choice does not affect learning 
outcomes; and (2) for computationally learned grammars, performance is 
strongest when grammars are induced over ARs under a graph-based 
categorical learner. These findings suggest that representation plays an 
important role in tonotactic learning.
mechanisms.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

This paper focuses on the learning of tonal phonotactics, or tonotactics, 
which addresses the question of the well-formedness of how tones and 
tone-bearing units (TBUs, such as syllable or mora) are organized in tonal 
languages, and explores how tonal representations
can influence the learning outcomes. For a long time, tonotactics has received less attention in computational learning than segmental phonotactics, despite the widespread distribution of tonal languages across the world \citep{yip2002tone}. One possible reason 
for this gap lies in representational differences. Segmental phonotactics is 
typically modeled using linear representations such as strings, which makes 
it readily compatible with a wide range of existing learning models. 
Tonotactics, by contrast, has long been analyzed over non-linear 
structures, most widely used autosegmental representations 
\citep[ARs,][]{goldsmith1976autosegmental}. This representational challenge  
has led to a dearth of computational models that operate directly over 
non-linear structures, with many approaches instead flattening tonal 
patterns into strings to fit linear learning frameworks. Although a substantial body of work has argued that linear representations are inadequate for capturing tonal generalizations \citep{clements2010autosegmental,oddenintroducing2013}, and other studies have examined the computational properties of ARs \citep{bird1990phonological,coleman1991no,kornai2007mathematical,jardine2015concatenation,jardine2016locality,jardine2017LocalNature,jardine2019expressivity}, empirical demonstrations of clear advantages for non-linear representations over strings in tonotactic learning remain relatively limited.

In this paper, we address two questions: (1) how effectively tonotactics can 
be learned over ARs, and (2) whether ARs improve learning outcomes 
compared with alternative string-based representations. To address these 
questions, we encoded the same dataset in both string and AR formats, and applied two 
versions of the Bottom-Up Factorial Inference Algorithm (BUFIA) 
\citep{chandleeBufia2019}: one operating over strings (BUFIA-Str) and the other directly 
over ARs (BUFIA-AR). As a reference, a set of linguistically 
motivated grammars, also encoded over both string and AR, serves as a 
baseline for comparison. Additionally, we include a probabilistic learner, 
the Maximum Entropy model \citep{hayes2008maximum}, applied to string 
representations (MaxEnt-Str), to examine whether the absence of AR structure 
can be compensated for by probabilistic modeling. A more comprehensive study 
should also include the version of 
MaxEnt operating over ARs. However, the current implementation of MaxEnt 
does not support such representations. Despite this limitation, 
this comparison still allows us to assess whether a structural 
representation will contribute to learning.

A cross-fold validation is conducted for comparing performance of models 
over varying representation. In two constructed test set, in addition to the 
positive data from the held-out set, negative forms of matched sizes
are also added into the test sets to see if models overgeneralize. 
The results yield three main findings. First, representational choice does 
not affect outcomes when the grammar is a fixed, static set of constraints, 
provided that the two representations stand in a one-to-one correspondence. 
For computationally learned grammars, the use of ARs improves learning outcomes,
with BUFIA-AR being the most robust model across conditions. This advantage is not offset by probabilistic modeling, which performs below the baseline.

Overall, this work highlights the importance of incorporating linguistically 
meaningful representations into computational models of phonological 
learning.

\section{Backgrounds}

This section outlines the theoretical background and computational 
methods for two representational choices: strings and ARs. We adopt a 
model-theoretic perspective that treats both representations as 
two types of mathematical objects \citep{enderton2001mathematical}, or more specifically, relational structures 
\citep{rogers2013,chandleeBufia2019,rogers.lambert2019mol}. This 
formalization makes it possible to represent ARs and provides the 
foundation for the learning models, that is, two versions of BUFIA, 
used in this study. It should be noted that the tonal representations 
studied here abstract away from segmental content and retain only tonal and 
TBU information. For example, the Hausa word \textit{mù.tûm} (``person'') can be 
represented linearly as L.HL, or as an AR in which each L, H, and L occupies 
one mora, with the first mora associated with the first syllable and the 
final two moras associated with the second syllable, respectively, as
we show later in Table 3.

\subsection{Linear vs.\ Non-linear Representations}

We define a phonological representation as relational structures of the 
form $\langle D, \mathcal{R} \rangle$, where $D$ is a finite domain of 
positions, and $\mathcal{R}$ is a set of relations (also known as the
model \textit{signature}) that connects the composing units of the 
phonological representation \citep{strother2017using, jardine2016locality}. Relations may be unary, 
indicating the identity of an element, or binary, specifying the 
relationship between pairs of elements. 

A string representation is single-tiered, and elements are arranged along
the one axis. The signature of a string model typically includes unary 
labeling relations indicating tone units, and a single binary successor 
relation $\lhd$ defining linear order. For example, \textit{L.HL} can be 
written and visualized as in Figure~\ref{sec2.fig.string}. The domain 
$D=\{1,2,3,4\}$ specifies four positions, labeled by tone and syllable 
boundary marker (.), and the successor relation encodes temporal order. The 
structural information (e.g., each tone occupying a mora) is encoded 
implicitly in the unary relations, for example by adding a boundary marker, 
or by allowing each symbol to implicitly represent one mora connected with 
one tone. For more complex tonal forms where a single TBU is associated with 
multiple tones, special symbols must be introduced into the alphabet, such 
as R (representing LH on one mora) or F (representing HL on one mora).

\input{string.model.tex}

ARs, in contrast, correspond to multi-tiered relational structures. The 
signature includes a successor relation but over multiple tiers 
(e.g., tone, mora, syllable). Associations across tiers are captured by an 
association relation $\alpha$. In this representation, structural 
dependencies such as tone spreading or contour formation are encoded 
directly as relations rather than inferred from symbol sequences. 

For example, in Figure~\ref{armodel}, the AR consists of eight units, 
defined by $D=\{1,\ldots,8\}$, arranged across three tiers, each following 
a successor relation $\lhd=\{(1,2),(2,3),\ldots\}$. The association 
relations, on the other hand, are expressed as a set of unordered pairs 
$\alpha=\{(1,4),(2,5),\ldots,(6,8)\}$, indicating which two elements are 
connected by an association line.

\input{AR.model.tex}

This formalization explicitly captures how within-tier and across-tier 
relations are represented in autosegmental phonology 
\citep{goldsmith1976autosegmental}. In particular, tonal generalizations 
have been argued to be computationally simpler over ARs than over strings, 
because cross-tier dependencies are represented directly as relations. This 
allows processes to be modeled by modifying associations rather than 
altering feature values or the alphabet, and can describe long-distance 
processes locally \citep{goldsmith1976autosegmental,jardine2017LocalNature}.

\subsection{Phonotactic Learning Models}

The main model used in this paper is based on the Bottom-Up 
Factor Inference Algorithm (BUFIA; \citet{chandleeBufia2019}). BUFIA takes 
on model-theoretic representations, thereby gives a flexibility of specific 
linguistic representations. In this work, we adopted two versions of BUFIA,
BUFIA-Str and BUFIA-AR.

In a nutshell, BUFIA is a categorical phonotactic learner that traverses a 
hypothesis space of all logically possible structures. These structures, 
which serve as candidate constraints, are partially ordered by a containment 
relation: some pairs of structures stand in a containment relation (one 
containing the other), while others are not comparable. As illustrated in 
Figure [maybe??] for strings over the alphabet ${a,b}$, more general 
structures (e.g., the empty string or singletons) occupy lower positions in 
the space, whereas more specific structures appear higher. For any given 
structure, those that properly contain it are its \textit{superfactors} 
(positioned above), and those that it properly contains are its 
\textit{subfactors} (positioned below). Among the superfactors, only those 
that are one level above the current candidate are referred to as its 
\textit{next superfactors}.

Two core operations of BUFIA determine its upward search or pruning: 
containment and the sub/superfactor relation. The learner is provided with a 
set of positive data and a complexity bound $k$. The search begins 
from an initial structure (typically the empty structure). For each 
candidate, BUFIA checks whether it is contained in the positive dataset. 
If it is attested, the learner generates its next superfactors and continues 
searching upward. Otherwise, the structure is identified as a forbidden 
factor (i.e., a constraint), and prune off its superfactors from space for 
not checking. In this way, BUFIA identifies the most general unattested 
structures and prunes all more specific structures that would necessarily 
also be forbidden. The resulting grammar guarantees full coverage of the 
observed data while remaining maximally general.

As said before, a key merit of BUFIA is its representational flexibility. 
The algorithm can operate over any representational choices, provided they 
are defined model-theoretically. Recent work has demonstrated the success of 
BUFIA in phonotactic learning. \citet{Swanson+2025} implemented a 
string-based version of BUFIA to Quechua phonotactics and showed that it 
captures the previous empirical generalizations. \citet{li2025learning} 
introduced a version of BUFIA that operates directly over ARs for tonotactic 
learning. This representational flexibility allows controlled comparison of 
different representations of the same data under a shared learning framework.

As noted above, BUFIA is a categorical learner: a candidate structure is 
either well-formed or ill-formed, depending on whether it contains any 
forbidden subfactor. In contrast, probabilistic models assign gradient 
well-formedness values. A representative of such models is the Maximum 
Entropy phonotactic learner \citep[MaxEnt;][]{hayes2008maximum}. MaxEnt 
interprets well-formedness as probability rather than a binary value and 
treats constraints as violable, each associated with a penalty weight. The 
well-formedness of a candidate is computed from the sum of its  
constraint violations. This evaluation is cumulative: the more constraints a 
candidate violates, and the higher the weights of those constraints, the 
lower its predicted probability of well-formedness.

MaxEnt has been widely used in string-based phonotactic learning, and more 
recent implementations can induce phonological tiers to capture non-local 
patterns \citep{wilson2018accidental,GouskovaGallagher2020}. 
\citet{lee2025representing} propose a MaxEnt-based learner for stress 
patterns that reflects metrical structure. These studies suggest that 
probabilistic string-based approaches can learn structural regularities that 
are not directly visible in surface sequences. However, these tier-based 
representations differ from ARs in that they typically do not encode 
association relations between individual elements on separate tiers.

The central question of this paper is whether tonotactics can be successfully 
learned from linear strings, or whether adopting ARs improves learning 
outcomes. A related question is whether learning outcomes are influenced by 
the type of learner. In the following section, we present an experiment 
designed to test these questions by applying the same dataset across 
different representations and two learning schemes.

\section{Experiment: Hausa Tonotactics}

To test tonotactic learnability across representations and models, 
we conducted a controlled computational experiment on the same dataset. 
In this section, we detail the experimental setup, 
including the dataset over two formats, the construction of test data, and 
the evaluation metrics used to compare model performance.

\subsection{Hausa Tonology}

The language data used in this study come from Hausa, a major tonal language spoken in 
West Africa. Hausa has three surface tones in orthography: H, L, and HL (a falling 
tone, which occurs only in heavy syllables).  Previous generalizations about Hausa
tonotactics are listed in Table~\ref{rules} \citep{Newmanbook, leben1996tonal, zoll2003optimal}.

\input{constraints.table.tex}

Among the constraints listed in Table~\ref{rules}, the first three constraints, 
\textsc{*Rise$_\sigma$},\textsc{*Contour$_{\mu}$},\textsc{*3T}, are hard constraints 
that define the basic phonology of tones and syllable structures of Hausa. 
Constraint \textsc{*NonFinHL} applies to the non-derived Hausa words where the falling
contour (HL) can only occur word finally. The constraint \textsc{*Lapse} is proposed 
by \citet{zoll2003optimal} that explain the rarity of L tone spreading. 
\citet{Newmanbook} points out that there are exceptional words that violate 
the last two constraints. 

These constraints are formalized over two representations as shown in 
Table~\ref{tab.rule.constraints}, and will serve as the \textit{baseline grammar} as 
a reference to which the learned grammar are compared.

\subsection{Data}
A curated corpus was constructed from the Hausa database \citep{wold-4} in the 
\textit{World Loanword Database} (WOLD, \citet{wold}). 
The curated corpus contains 621 monomorphemic, non-borrowed words from the core 
vocabulary, transcribed in orthography. We chose these forms to make sure learning 
results are in the comparable morphological categories as the previously established 
generalizations. The 621 forms are converted into two representations, yielding 64 
unique types with a one-to-one AR-string correspondence. Table~\ref{sec4.tab.conversion} provides two examples of these 
representations. 

\input{reps.examples.tex}

For example, the string representation of word \textit{kòò.gíí} `river' is represented as \textit{LL.HH}.
The double L or H indicate the syllable is heavy. The syllable boundary is indicated 
by a period. Contour tones are decomposed into level tones; thus, the falling tone in 
\textit{mù.tûm} `person' is represented as \textit{HL}. Without assuming any prior 
tonal knowledge, we additionally introduce \textit{R} and \textit{F} into 
the alphabet to represent monosyllabic LH and monosyllabic HL tones, respectively.

Unlike the string representation, AR does not require expanding the tonal inventory 
to include symbols such as R and F, but needs to incorporate 
mora and syllables ($\mu,\sigma$), since contours are represented as multiple tones 
associating with a TBU. Consequently, syllable 
boundaries are naturally encoded in the AR structure. In the implementation, each AR is encoded as a pair of adjacency matrices, which we do not discuss further here.

\subsection{Procedure}

The experiment compares grammars learned under three conditions (BUFIA-AR, BUFIA-Str, 
and MaxEnt-Str) and evaluates them against baseline grammars defined over the 
corresponding representations (Baseline-AR and Baseline-Str). A flowchart illustrating 
the experimental setup is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:concept-map}.

The positive data are converted into two representational formats. For the string 
representation, the data are used as input to MaxEnt-Str and BUFIA-Str. In both cases, 
the input strings are encoded as feature-based sequences using a reduced tonal 
inventory consisting of H, L, R, F, and a syllable boundary marker. Because the 
baseline grammar targets disyllabic patterns, the BUFIA-Str learner is constrained by a 
complexity threshold of $k=5$ (e.g., XX.XX), corresponding to the form of a disyllabic 
form.

For the AR representation, the data are provided to BUFIA-AR which 
requires three complexity parameters to the three tiers: $t$ 
(maximum tone units), $m$ (maximum mora units), and $s$ (maximum syllable units). To 
approximate the same disyllabic form used in the string condition, we chose
$t=2, s = 2, m =4$. We are aware that the two complexity settings cannot 
be made fully equivalent, since AR complexity is distributed across 
multiple tiers rather than captured by a single linear bound. 
Nonetheless, this attempt to align the thresholds aims to control the 
learning conditions across representational formats as closely as possible.

\subsection{Evaluation}
We conducted a $4$-fold cross-validation procedure, partitioning the dataset 
into four equally sized folds. In each iteration, three folds are used for 
training and the remaining fold serves as part of the test sets. This process 
is repeated four times, with each fold acting as the test set once.


During evaluation, we do not test only on the held-out positive data but also construct two types of test sets: the \textit{regular set} and 
the \textit{extended set}. The \textit{regular set} consists of the 
held-out positive forms (16 types) 
together with an equal number of manually constructed negative forms (32 types
in total). Each constructed negative form violates at least one of the baseline 
constraints. For each condition, the test items are encoded in the 
corresponding representation and evaluated together with 
the positive data. This design allows us to assess whether the learned 
grammar over generates beyond the attested patterns.
The \textit{extended set} builds on the regular set by adding four 
exceptional positive types and four matched negative forms (40 types in total). 
The exceptional forms are drawn from Hausa data 
reported by Newman and violate both \textsc{*Lapse} and 
\textsc{*NonFinHL}. 

In model evaluation, a well-formedness judgment is counted as correct if the 
model accepts attested examples and rejects the negative examples. 
Performance is measured in terms of recall, precision, and F1 score over these 
classifications.

It should be noted that the \text{MaxEnt} model returns a gradient 
grammar in which all constraints are violable and assigned weights. 
However, for comparability with the categorical models, we 
convert the learned  MaxEnt grammar into a categorical 
interpretation: candidate forms whose total weighted violation score 
is zero are treated as well-formed, whereas those with a non-zero 
weighted sum are treated as ill-formed. In other words, a test candidate 
is classified as well-formed only if it does not violate any constraint 
in the grammar.

The averaged results across folds are shown in Table~\ref{tab:results-merged}. For both test sets, all 
models under both representations achieved perfect precision (1.000). This indicates 
that, regardless of the learning mechanism, both string and AR representations 
successfully reject the constructed ill-formed structures.


First, a comparison between baseline-Str and baseline-AR shows that their results are 
identical. When the same grammar is applied to the two representations, it produces 
exactly the same predictions on the dataset. A closer inspection of the errors 
confirms that they are identical as well. It shows that, when there is a one-to-one 
correspondence between representations, the difference in representation alone does 
not affect the behavior of a fixed grammar over surface forms. However, to ensure a 
one-to-one mapping between string and AR, the alphabets of the two system are 
made different. The string alphabet includes R, F, and syllable boundary markers, 
whereas the AR representation includes mora and syllable.


In contrast to the baseline grammar, recall and F1 scores for the learned grammars 
show greater variation. Across both test sets, the same performance pattern emerges:
\textbf{BUFIA-AR > BUFIA-Str > Baseline > MaxEnt-Str}.

Comparing the two versions of the BUFIA learner, the choice of representation clearly 
affects learning outcomes. Specifically, BUFIA-Str achieves an F1 score of 0.950 on 
the regular test set and 0.903 on the extended test set. When switching to ARs, 
the F1 score increases to 0.992 on the regular set and 0.994 on the 
extended set. With the learning algorithm held constant, this improvement indicates 
that AR representations yield more accurate predictions for unseen attested forms. 

Finally, we evaluated MaxEnt-Str. Its learning performance is lower than that of all 
other models, including the baseline grammar.


\section{Discussion and Conclusion}

We see from the results that as for a surface-based phonotactics, different 
representations can have equal generalization capacity if the they 
are equivalent. \citet{oakdenNotationalEquivalenceTonal2020} argues that different
representations are notationally equivalent if there exists a set of bijective 
transduction from one system to the other. \citet{danis2025logical} further argues notational equivalence further requires that one system to persevere all possible nature classes that 
other predicts, and vice versa. Here, we do not claim that the two representations are 
equivalent. Rather, we propose that computational models with representational 
flexibility, such as BUFIA, can be used as practical comparison tool 
for evaluating representational equivalence by examining whether learning outcomes 
remain consistent across representations. Besides, as mentioned earlier, the two 
systems rely on different alphabets, that is, different basic units of phonological 
representation. One could ask how these units are determined and whether they can be 
supported by computational learning or by psycholinguistic evidence.

Beyond the baseline grammar, comparisons among computationally learned, dynamic 
grammars show that the two BUFIA learners differ in performance. Learning over ARs 
achieves higher accuracy on attested forms without increased overgeneralization. This 
pattern remains robust even when the dataset includes rare forms.

The relatively low performance of the MaxEnt model may raise questions. One possible 
explanation is the limited dataset size. This
study used type-based rather than token-based data. 
Without large dataset or frequency information, the 
MaxEnt performance may be limited. In addition, we 
interpret all MaxEnt constraints categorically: any 
non-zero weighted violation sum, even if very 
small, is treated as a violation. Future work could 
explore how to determine well-formedness thresholds 
more systematically.

In conclusion, we show that tonotactics can be learned effectively and efficiently over ARs using BUFIA-AR, even without frequency information. This highlights a future direction for grammar inference in tonal languages. For example, many tonal processes (input–output mappings) involve changes in association relations rather than changes in symbols, which makes learning over multi-tier structures more necessary. More generally, adopting linguistically interpretable learning models  
or representations will also help distinguish among competing theories of linguistic representation and generalization.
