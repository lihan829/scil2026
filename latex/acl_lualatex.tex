\begin{abstract}
This paper investigates whether phonological learning primarily arises from the data representation (string vs. autosegmental representations) or from the learning model (probabilistic vs. categorical). We compare two learning frameworks: a Maximum Entropy (MaxEnt) model and BUFIA, a categorical learner adapted to operate over ARs. Using the same training and testing datasets across conditions, we systematically evaluate four combinations: MaxEnt-Ftr, BUFIA-Ftr, and BUFIA-AR. The results show that learning performance is strongest when grammars are induced over ARs, particularly when paired with a categorical learner. These findings suggest that representational structure plays a role in learning success than probabilistic weighting, and that categorical structure-sensitive learners benefit most from autosegmental representations.
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Data}

The dataset is a curated corpus derived from the Hausa entries in the \textit{World Loanword Database}. We restrict attention to monomorphemic, non-borrowed forms.
 After filtering, the dataset contains 621 lexical iterms (64 unique tonal types), transcribed in standard orthography and stored in a CSV format. Each word was converted into both a string-based tonal representation and an autosegmental representation encoding tone–mora–syllable associations.

BUFIA achieves substantially higher recall on attested forms, while the Newman grammar is more conservative and rejects a larger portion of exceptional or marginal patterns. This tradeoff reflects differences in inductive bias: the positive-only learner prioritizes coverage of observed structures, whereas the hand-specified grammar encodes strong a priori restrictions that reduce overgeneration.
